/ The story

Marketing Anatomy started in 2022 as a side practice — Shweta Kukreja was running personal branding for two founders, both too busy to write but too sharp to delegate to a "social media manager."

What began as a workaround turned into a method: study the founder for two weeks, write in their voice, ship daily, defend strategy with data. The accounts grew. Inbound followed. Other founders asked.

Today Marketing Anatomy is a small studio of strategists, writers and designers serving founders, operators and investors. We don't take on every client. We take on the ones whose work is interesting enough that we'd want to be telling the story.

/ The method

We treat a personal brand the way you'd treat a product. There's a thesis, a hypothesis about who the audience is, a content roadmap, and a weekly review where we look at what worked and rewrite what didn't.

It's slower than a content factory. It's also why the work compounds.

§ How we work

A few opinions, held loosely.

/ 01

Voice over volume.

One post you'd save beats fifteen that sound like LinkedIn. We'd rather skip a day than ship slop.

/ 02

Strategy is a verb.

Strategy isn't a deck. It's the question we ask before every post: who is this for, what does it earn, why now?

/ 03

Receipts > promises.

You'll see real numbers in your weekly report. Engagement, inbound, sentiment. We name what worked and what didn't.

/ 04

Boring tools, sharp ideas.

Notion for ops. Google Docs for drafts. Native composers for posting. Energy goes to the writing.

/ 05

The founder is the brand.

You can't outsource taste. We can write the words and own the ops — but the point of view stays yours.

/ 06

Long horizon, short loops.

Personal brands compound on a 12–24 month timeline. We work in weekly loops, but plan for the long game.

Issue 046 — The Anatomy Newsletter Apr 21, 2026

Why your second post matters more than your first.

"The first post gets you noticed. The second decides whether anyone stays."

Most founders obsess over the hook. The opening line. The thumbnail of the carousel. And they should — about half of LinkedIn's algorithm decides in the first 1.3 seconds whether to keep showing your post.

But the hook is a one-time event. After someone has read your hook, clicked your profile, and decided you might be interesting — they look at the second post on your feed.

That post does a different job. It's not trying to win attention. It's trying to earn trust.

Here's what we tell every founder: your second post should answer one question — "what does this person know that I don't?" Not opinions. Not takes. A specific, hard-won piece of knowledge. The kind of thing you'd only know after shipping for ten years.

Three patterns we've seen work, with examples from our portfolio:

Pattern 1: The unglamorous fact. "We spent 3 months on a feature 14 customers asked for. Two used it." Nobody can manufacture that — it's real, specific, and disarming.

Pattern 2: The reversal. "Everyone says X. We've actually found Y." Take the consensus, demonstrate why it's wrong with one concrete example.

Pattern 3: The receipt. "We sent 1,247 cold emails last quarter. Here's what 14% reply rates actually looked like." Numbers + context = built-in credibility.

If you want our full carousel of hook patterns, it's in next week's issue. Hit reply with what you're working on — I read every email.

— Shweta
